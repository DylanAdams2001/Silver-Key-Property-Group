01 — Overview

An heirloom, not a start-up

Silver Key exists to make an unglamorous asset class — converted, multi-let houses — read as seriously as any institutional property fund. The brand borrows its authority from private banking and heritage estate agency, not proptech.

Positioning statement

Silver Key Property Group is the UK conversion partner of Keyspace Property Group (Australia). Where Keyspace builds from the ground up, Silver Key works with what's already standing — sourcing under-let period houses, converting them into licensed HMOs, and managing them for long-term room-by-room income.

Personality

  • Established, not flashy. The brand should feel decades old on day one — restrained colour, classic serif type, formal composition.
  • Precise, not hyped. Every claim is a number, a timeframe, or a licence — never an adjective doing the work a fact should do.
  • Warm, not corporate. Bottle green and parchment read as private and considered, not as a bank's navy-and-grey.

Clear space & minimum size

Keep clear space on all sides equal to at least half the mark's diameter (dashed line, for reference only — do not reproduce the dashed box). Never render the mark alone smaller than 24px / 18mm; below that, use the wordmark without the seal.

Do / Don't

Do

  • Use the three approved colour variants only (ink, reversed, foil).
  • Keep the roundel perfectly circular — never stretch or skew.
  • Place on a solid panel when sitting over a photograph.

Don't

  • Rotate the key or tilt the roundel.
  • Add drop shadows, gradients, or bevels to the mark.
  • Recolour it outside the palette on page 3.

04 — Typography

One serif family, two roles

Display and body both come from the same classic-serif world, kept apart only by size, weight, and the occasional italic — deliberately avoiding a display/body pairing that reads as "modern tech."

Display face: 'Hoefler Text', Georgia, 'Times New Roman', serif
Body face: Georgia, 'Iowan Old Style', 'Times New Roman', serif
Hoefler Text ships with macOS; Georgia is universal on Windows and Mac, so the fallback still reads as classic serif rather than collapsing to a default sans. Do not substitute a grotesk/sans-serif anywhere in this system — that single change undoes the heritage positioning faster than any colour mistake.

Rules of use

  • Italic, not bold, for emphasis. Bottle-green italics carry the one or two words that matter in a headline.
  • All-caps only for tiny labels. Eyebrows and captions may be uppercase with wide letter-spacing (0.12–0.16em); nothing larger than 14px should ever be set in all-caps.
  • Numerals are tabular and set in the display face, in gold, wherever a genuine sequence or statistic appears.

05 — Imagery

Real photography, one consistent treatment

Photograph real period property and communal interiors — never generic new-build stock or lifestyle/people photography. Every image runs through the same duotone treatment before it goes anywhere near the brand.

Treatment recipe
  1. Desaturate to roughly 80% of original saturation.
  2. Apply a warm sepia tone (light, not full brown-and-white — the photo should still read as a real place).
  3. Increase contrast slightly (~8%) and pull brightness down a touch (~4%) so nothing in the frame is paper-white.
  4. Lay a linear gradient over the top — Bottle Green at ~20% opacity in one corner fading to Gold at ~14% opacity in the opposite corner — set to Multiply blend mode.
CSS equivalent, for reference: filter: sepia(.32) saturate(.8) contrast(1.08) brightness(.96); plus a linear-gradient(160deg, rgba(30,59,44,.2), rgba(169,131,46,.14)) overlay at mix-blend-mode: multiply.

Subject matter

Photograph

  • Period terrace and townhouse exteriors — brick, stone, sash windows.
  • Communal kitchens, dining, and living spaces, well-lit and lived-in.
  • Clean architectural floor plans as line drawings, not photos.

Avoid

  • Glossy new-build show-home photography.
  • Lifestyle/people-focused shots — keep the focus on the asset.
  • Anything already carrying a strong colour cast the treatment can't correct for.

07 — Voice & tone

Precise, plain, never hyped

Silver Key talks like a prospectus, not an advert. Every claim is backed by a number, a timeframe, or a named process step.

Do10–14 weeks, acquisition to first let.
Don'tLightning-fast conversions that will blow your mind!
DoTarget net yield of 7–9% per annum.
Don'tGuaranteed sky-high returns.
DoPre-checked against Article 4 and local licensing requirements.
Don'tNo red tape, we handle it all!

Ground rules

  • Active voice, specific numbers. Replace adjectives ("amazing yield") with the figure itself.
  • Forward-looking figures are always "target" or "indicative." Never state a projected return as a fact.
  • Capital-at-risk language appears wherever returns are discussed, in the same understated tone as the rest of the copy — not as a buried legal footnote in a different voice.
